Oncotype DX Breast Recurrence Score

Exact Sciences
Genomic + AI breast

21-gene expression assay for recurrence risk and chemotherapy benefit prediction in hormone receptor-positive, HER2-negative early-stage breast cancer.

Regulatory

Regulatory statusLDT
LDTYes

Laboratory-developed test (LDT) offered under CLIA. Not reviewed or cleared by the FDA. Validation is the responsibility of the offering CLIA-certified laboratory.

Validation summary

Study designRandomized controlled trial
Cohort size10,273 patients — TAILORx enrolled 10,273 women with HR+/HER2-/N0 breast cancer (2006–2010); 9,719 eligible with follow-up; primary analysis on the 6,711 (69%) with midrange Recurrence Score 11–25 randomly assigned to chemoendocrine vs endocrine-alone.
Number of sites—
Site geographyMulti-center (international)
ComparatorClinical outcomes
Primary endpointtime_to_event_risk_strata
Primary result9-year iDFS rate in midrange RS 11–25: endocrine alone 83.3% vs chemoendocrine 84.3% (HR 1.08; 95% CI 0.94–1.24; non-inferiority criterion met). 9-year distant-recurrence rate: endocrine 4.9% vs chemoendocrine 4.6%. Subgroup signal of chemo benefit in women ≤50 years with RS 16–25. For RS 0–15: 9-yr distant recurrence 3% irrespective of age (endocrine alone).
External validationYes — TAILORx is itself a prospective external validation of the Recurrence Score categories originally derived from NSABP B-14 / B-20 archived cohorts; ~1,200 US/Canada/Australia/Ireland/Peru sites. · Confirmed prognostic stratification and lack of chemo benefit for RS 11–25 overall.
Peer-reviewedYes
FDA decision summary—

Key publications

  • Adjuvant Chemotherapy Guided by a 21-Gene Expression Assay in Breast Cancer pivotal
    New England Journal of Medicine · 2018

Limitations noted

TAILORx primary analysis applies only to women with RS 11–25; women with RS 0–10 (assigned endocrine alone) and RS 26–100 (assigned chemoendocrine) were not randomized. Race/ethnicity subgroup analyses underpowered for non-White populations. Test is LDT; analytical validation rests on prior NSABP archival-cohort studies (Paik 2004 NEJM, Paik 2006 JCO).
